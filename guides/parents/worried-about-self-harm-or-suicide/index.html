I'm worried my child may be self-harming or thinking about suicide

What to look for, how to start the conversation, and exactly what to do — from a clinician who has sat with families in these moments many times.

If you're reading this right now

You're scared. Maybe your child said something that stopped you cold. Maybe you found something — a text, a mark on their arm, a journal entry. Maybe it's just a feeling you can't shake, and you don't know if you're overreacting or not seeing enough.

Whatever brought you here, I want you to know: the fact that you're asking this question means you're paying attention. That matters more than you know.

This guide is written to give you what you actually need — not reassurance that everything is fine, and not panic. Just clear, honest information so you can take the right next step.

Self-harm vs. suicidal thinking: understanding the difference

These two things often get talked about together, and they can overlap — but they are not the same thing, and understanding the difference helps you respond to each one appropriately.

Self-harm (non-suicidal self-injury)

Self-harm — most commonly cutting, but also burning, hitting, or scratching — is typically not an attempt to end one's life. In most cases, it functions as a way to cope with overwhelming emotional pain that a young person doesn't yet have the tools to manage differently. It is a signal that your child is suffering and needs support, but it does not automatically mean they want to die.

That said, self-harm should always be taken seriously. It is not a phase to wait out, and it is not attention-seeking in the dismissive sense of that phrase — it is a young person communicating that something is very wrong.

Suicidal thinking

Suicidal thinking exists on a spectrum. On one end is passive ideation — thoughts like "I wish I wasn't here" or "Everyone would be better off without me" — which, while serious, is different from active planning. On the other end is active ideation with a plan, which is a psychiatric emergency requiring immediate action.

Both ends of this spectrum deserve a response. The difference is the urgency and the type of response needed.

Important to know

Self-harm and suicidal thinking can co-exist. A young person who self-harms may also have thoughts of suicide. This is why any disclosure of self-harm should include a direct, calm conversation about suicidal thinking as well.

Warning signs to take seriously

No single sign is definitive on its own. What you're looking for is a pattern of change — especially changes that are new, persistent, or intensifying.

Behavioral changes

  • Withdrawal from friends, family, and activities they used to enjoy
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Saying goodbye in ways that feel unusual or final
  • Increased secrecy, especially around their body (long sleeves in summer, avoiding situations where skin is visible)
  • Researching methods of suicide or self-harm
  • A sudden, unexplained calm after a period of depression — this can sometimes indicate a decision has been made

What they're saying

  • "I wish I was dead" or "I want to kill myself" — even if said casually or in anger, take it seriously
  • "I can't do this anymore"
  • "Nobody would care if I was gone"
  • "I just want the pain to stop"
  • "Things would be better without me"

Emotional signs

  • Hopelessness — especially statements that the future won't get better
  • Expressions of feeling trapped or like there's no way out
  • Extreme emotional swings, or a flatness that is uncharacteristic
  • Intense shame, guilt, or feeling like a burden

A note on risk factors

Certain experiences increase risk and are worth knowing about: a previous attempt, a recent loss or rejection, LGBTQ+ youth facing unsupportive environments, a family history of suicide, access to lethal means (especially firearms), and substance use. None of these mean something will happen — but each one is a reason to take warning signs more seriously, not less.

The question parents are afraid to ask

One of the most persistent and damaging myths in mental health is this: if you ask a child about suicide, you might plant the idea.

This is not true. Research consistently shows the opposite — asking directly about suicide does not increase risk, and for many young people, being asked directly is a profound relief. It signals that you can handle the truth, and that they don't have to carry it alone anymore.

What does increase risk is the opposite: leaving the question unasked, communicating — even unintentionally — that this topic is too scary or shameful to discuss.

Ask directly

The most effective question is simple and specific: "Are you thinking about suicide?" Not "You're not thinking about hurting yourself, are you?" — which invites a "no." Not "Are you okay?" — which is too easy to deflect. Ask directly, and then be quiet and listen.

How to have the conversation

You don't need to be a therapist to have this conversation well. You need to be present, calm, and willing to hear whatever comes.

Before you start

  • Choose a private, low-pressure setting — a car ride is often ideal because there's no eye contact and neither person can leave easily
  • Put your phone away
  • Prepare yourself to stay calm regardless of what you hear — your child will be watching your reaction closely to decide how much to share

What to say

Start with what you've noticed, not with an accusation or alarm: "I've noticed you seem like you're carrying something heavy lately, and I want to make sure I'm not missing anything. Are you thinking about suicide or hurting yourself?"

If they say yes, or say something that sounds like yes: "Thank you for telling me. I'm not going anywhere. Can you tell me more about what that's been like for you?"

If they deny it but you're still concerned: "Okay. I hear you. But I want you to know that if that ever changes, or if things get darker, I want to be the person you come to. I can handle it."

What not to say

  • Don't say "You have so much to live for" — this can feel dismissive and increase shame
  • Don't say "That would destroy our family" — this places the burden of your wellbeing on them
  • Don't promise to keep it a secret — you may need to act on what they tell you
  • Don't panic visibly — it will shut the conversation down
  • Don't immediately jump to problem-solving — listen first

What to do based on what you're seeing

The right next step depends on what's actually happening. Here's a clear framework:

Immediate danger

Your child has a plan, has access to means, or has already made an attempt. They may be unreachable, unresponsive, or have taken something.

Call 911 immediately. Do not leave them alone. Remove access to any means you can safely control (medications, sharp objects, firearms).

Active crisis, not immediate

Your child has disclosed active suicidal thinking, with or without a specific plan, but is physically safe right now.

Call or text 988 together, or take them to the nearest emergency department. Do not leave them alone. Contact their therapist or prescriber if they have one.

Concerning but stable

Your child has expressed passive ideation ("I wish I wasn't here"), is self-harming without suicidal intent, or you're seeing warning signs but no disclosure.

Schedule a mental health evaluation as soon as possible — ideally within days, not weeks. Increase your presence at home. Remove or secure lethal means. Keep the conversation open.

On removing access to means

This is one of the most evidence-based interventions that exists — and one of the most underused. If there are firearms in the home, they should be stored outside the home during this period, or locked with ammunition stored separately. Medications should be inventoried and secured. This is not a permanent measure — it is a bridge while your child gets support. The research is clear: reducing access to means saves lives.

Common myths that get in the way

Kids who talk about suicide are just looking for attention.
All communication about suicide deserves a response. "Attention-seeking" is a phrase that has caused real harm — because it has led parents and clinicians to dismiss disclosures that turned out to be genuine. Every statement about suicide is a statement that someone is in pain. Respond accordingly.
If they were really serious, they wouldn't tell anyone.
Most people who die by suicide communicated their intent beforehand — to a friend, a family member, or in writing. Disclosure is often a call for connection and intervention, not evidence that nothing will happen.
They cut themselves to get attention, not because they're really hurting.
Self-harm is a symptom of real emotional pain, regardless of what function it serves. The fact that it may get attention doesn't make the pain less real or the behavior less serious.
Asking about suicide will make them think about it.
Research consistently shows this is false. Asking directly about suicide does not increase suicidal thinking. For most young people, being asked directly — calmly and without judgment — provides relief.

After the conversation: what comes next

Whether your child disclosed something serious or you're still not sure, there are several things worth doing in the days that follow.

Get a professional assessment

A single conversation with you — however good — is not a substitute for a professional evaluation. A mental health clinician can assess risk more systematically, identify what's driving what you're seeing, and recommend the right level of care. If your child already has a therapist, contact them immediately. If they don't, this is the time to get one — and "as soon as possible" means within days, not the next available opening in six weeks.

Increase your presence

This doesn't mean surveillance — it means showing up more. More meals together. More check-ins that aren't about the problem ("I just wanted to sit with you"). More explicit expressions that you're not going anywhere. Research on adolescent resilience consistently points to parental connection as one of the most protective factors that exists.

Take care of yourself

This is not a footnote. Parents who discover their child is suicidal or self-harming often experience their own acute distress — shock, guilt, helplessness, and fear that can be overwhelming. You will be more useful to your child if you have support too. That might mean a therapist, a trusted friend, or a parent support group. It does not mean handling this alone.

A word on guilt

Parents in this situation almost universally ask: "Did I miss something? Did I cause this?" The honest answer is that mental illness and suicidal thinking are not caused by any single parenting decision. You are not the reason this is happening. The fact that you're here, reading this, trying to figure out how to help — that is what good parenting looks like in a hard moment.
